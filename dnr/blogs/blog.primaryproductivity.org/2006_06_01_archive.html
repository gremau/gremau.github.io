Trials and tribulations of field botanists in the "Year of the Astragalus"

Intrepid botanists on the road back to camp for the night.

People, I'm really trying to stay above the once a week blogging threshold. But alas, it can be difficult to come up with witty stories and mind-boggling photographs on a regular basis, especially while assuming the herculean task of finding and collecting huge amounts of native plant seed in the far flung locales of the Beehive State. I hope that the unbelievably high quality of the individual posts will make up for the sometimes painfully long (for the reader) periods in between them.

Now to the subject of today's post:

Matthew and I rocked the proverbial house last week on a two day collecting stint in the Swell. We collected another two members of Astragalus -praelongus and moencoppensis- for Kew, got a huge collection of Singleleaf ash for the Bend seed bank, and a somewhat disappointing collection from Poliomintha incana. The kicker though, was a whopping 10 collections that will go to the Red Butte Garden seed bank for trials. Granted, these are small collections and it wasn't that hard to get 10 done, but they are all fantastic species with great horticultural potential. With any luck you will be seeing them in the RB Gardens in the next few years. With even more luck, some may make it out of Red Butte Gardens and into the gardens of the world - maybe even yours - in the years to come.

Here are a few of my faves for the trial program:

Linum puberulum, a beautiful, delicate little flax growing in open Pinion-Juniper table-lands.

Eriogonum bicolor, Pretty buckwheat. I can't believe I havent shown a pic of this in bloom yet.

Syphoricarpos longiflorus, Longflower snowberry. A big, flowing shrub that should do well in hot, dry shade.

Closing thoughts:
Considering the enormous success we're having collecting Astragalus, I'd like to name 2006 the "Year of the Astragalus." If 2007 is as favorable as I'm hoping it will be, I'd like to call it the "Year of the Eriogonum." I think it would be great to have this become a tradition for everyone at Red Butte (at least), with a new genus introduced and celebrated with great fanfare every May. Perhaps there could even be a parade down the Floral Walk as the opening event for the plant sale or something. Will someone bring this up at the next staff meeting for me please?

This blog: Is it the future of the botanical media?

There has been much discussion lately about the emerging blogosphere's imminent role in revolutionizing "the news" and overtaking the "mainsteam media" as the public's main source of information about the world around them. Though we here at the "; plants of various habit and distribution........" blog elected NOT to attend the first annual meeting of the progressive blogosphere at the YearlyKos convention in Las Vegas, NV last week, we would nonetheless like to add our two cents to all the fuss. We see ourselves as martyrs for "the cause", and given the staggering growth in readership since our inception, we think the entire world will take note.

In a sense, we owe a debt of gratitude to the "mainstream media" for ignoring the key issues, such as invasive plant species, the phenomenon known as "locoweeds", human encroachment into native plant communities, and the effects of global warming (pdf) that are already being felt in ecosystems around the globe. Their ineptitude has opened a whole universe of crucial issues for us to report and offer carefully considered editorials on. Though we too will mourn the disappearance of current events printed on old fashioned dead plant fibers with (sometimes) soy derived inks, we hope that the tradeoff will be for accuracy in reporting on issues that matter to plants, and by association, all the organisms in this community that we know as Earth.

You, our loyal readers, can rest assured knowing that this blog will continue take the lead in reporting most current, prescient, and accurate stories in the plant world. Thanks for reading.

Tuesday, June 13, 2006

Visiting the Desert Experimental Range

Enter the Desert Experimental Range - no human-animal hybrids here. Tunnel Springs Range in background.

When I saw a "Desert Experimental Range" on the map, sprawled out in a desolate valley near the Utah-Nevada border, I had visions of top secret flying machines and pens full of human-animal hybrids. Of course I had to find out the truth, and finding out if the place could somehow be productive for the Seeds of Success program was the perfect in. The truth about the Desert Experimental Range, which is not classified, secret, or scary in any way, is this: it is a 225 square km plot of land that has been set aside for range management and ecology research. The research done here can help improve land management techniques for the 200,000 square km of land in the Great Basin that are floristically and geologically similar to the DER.

Prunus fasciculata, Desert almond. Evidently, fruit production is rare here.

The DER was set aside in 1933 by President Herbert Hoover. At this time, as the Great Depression was beginning, the public lands of the West and especially the Great Basin had been under intensive use for grazing for some time. This unmanaged use was beginning to take a toll on the landscape, the health of the plant communities, and the productivity of the region's livestock industry. Hoover set aside the DER as an "agricultural range experiment station" to encourage research into rangeland ecology of the Great Basin, and to develop more sustainable livestock practices. To this end, the DER was fenced by the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC) in 1935 and portions of the land were divided into various enclosures and exclosures so that grazing regimes and management techniques could be tested and the results observed on a long term basis.
"Sheep grazing studies began in the winter of 1934/1935 to study the economic and ecological impacts of grazing at different intensities, seasons, and frequencies. The core of those grazing treatments has been maintained each winter for 59 years. Cattle grazing was added later. Several permanent exclosures ranging in size from 0.4 to 740 ha (1.0 to 1828 acres) are scattered throughout the Desert Experimental Range on all plant community types permitting direct examination of the effects of grazing and nonuse." --from Desert Experimental Range publication, Stanley G. Kitchen and E. Durant McArthur.

DER post office. Wah Wah Mountains in distance.

In1976 the DER was incorporated into the UNESCO Man and the Biosphere programme's network of reserves, representing the worlds cold-desert biomes. The purpose of MAB reserves is to encourage research on how humankind interacts with its environment with goals of reducing biodiversity loss, improving livelihoods, and enhancing social, economic and cultural conditions for environmental sustainability. The DER is one of approximately 480 reserves set aside in 100 countries around the world to represent all the world's biomes.
"The Man and the Biosphere Programme (MAB), proposes an interdisciplinary research agenda and capacity building aiming to improve the relationship of people with their environment globally. Launched in the early 1970s, it notably targets the ecological, social and economic dimensions of biodiversity loss and the reduction of this loss. It uses its World Network of Biosphere Reserves as vehicles for knowledge-sharing, research and monitoring, education and training, and participatory decision-making." -- From UNESCO's MAB website.
Thanks to Stan for making my visit more productive.

Monday, June 12, 2006

A hot harvest, while sniffing the winds of change

Kevin and 20,000 Astragalus rafaelensis seeds, Calf Canyon, UT

Its getting to be harvest time! Just returned from a brilliant 2 day trip to the Swell with some members of our fine RBG Volunteer Corps. Together we harvested 3 Kew collections - Astragalus rafaelensis, Astragalus woodruffi, and Gaillardia spathulata. Many thanks to Jenn, Jake, and Kevin for all the help out there under the unforgiving sun.

We also got a smaller collection of Poliomintha incana done, but we might have to try again later for a larger collection of this one. It has a lovely minty sage scent, making it a pleasure to collect, and outstanding horticultural potential, but it also has the difficult job of stabilizing sand dunes in a very windy area. Given this valuable ecological role and the ongoing process of climate change, it may be an increasingly important species to collect for restoration purposes. I'm going to put together an educational post about eolian (windblown) sand, vegetation, and climate change soon, but as a teaser, see this feature on dust in Utah from NPR. Hopefully all the Hoary Desert Rosemary-mint seed wont get sandblasted away in the next few weeks.

At other plots, stretched around the Equator like a belt, plot science takes on Indiana Jones dimensions.
