Dodecatheon utahensis expedition - Year of Our Lord 2006

Last month, Matthew Utley, David Compton, Tara Poelzing, and I found a new population of a rare plant in Big Cottonwood Canyon. Dodecatheon utahensis (formerly Dodecatheon dentatum var. utahensis) grows only in a few locations in the canyon. It is a striking, magical little being that lives along seeps and waterfalls. We had a harrowing day finding it, but it all ended up being worthwhile.

Here are my journal entries for the day.


7:00 am:

It is a beautiful, sunny August morning in Big Cottonwood Canyon. The survey crew of 7 of the most eminent botanists in the region has assembled at the base of the trial to Lake Blanch and there is animated discussion and convivial laugther while we drink our morning coffee and prepare to embark on our grand adventure in search of the rare and beautiful Dodecatheon utahensis.

12:00 pm:

After reaching the lake we split into 3 search parties and begin searching crevices and seeping overhangs in the quartzite cliff bands descending from the Sundial into the lake. After much searching there is still no sign of the Dodecatheon. Our energy is diminished, but spirits remain high and there is optimistic banter about finding our plant.

2:00 pm:

Still nothing, and there are now some good natured arguments over routfinding and debates on what search areas to take on in order to make the most of the day. Rations are running low and some members of the crew begin to mention their afternoon engagements. Overall the mood is good though.

2:45pm:

Half of the Dodecatheon search party turns back. The rest of us try to keep a positive attitude.

3:30 pm:

It has become quite hot this afternoon and most of our water bottles are dry. There is more grumbling among the crew. Someone cracks a joke about mutiny. Uncomfortable laughter.

4:25 pm:

The crew is now exhausted and patience is at an end. Some crew members are audibly and openly discussing mutiny. For my part, I have a swollen tongue, a cottony mouth, and am feeling very weary. I'm ready to give up. Someone mentions that Mr. David Compton parted with the group a while back.

4:45 pm:

Mr. Compton has been missing for some 30 minutes now. Our search has left us emptyhanded, impatient, and dehydrated by this point and skeptics among us have now decided that the element occurrence that we are trying to verify was mistaken and that we are now on a wild goose chase with one unaccounted-for botanist already and more on the way if we don't turn around. There is also a faction of the group expressing frustration for Mister Compton and at some point the consensus becomes that he must be taught a lesson about "not being a douche and staying with the group" The crew makes plans to desert without him.

4:46 pm:

Mr. Compton is sighted high above the crew, just beneath Sundial peak. He is gibbering unintelligibly and seems to be gesturing for the rest of the crew to hike up 1000 more vertical feet to join him. This is fiercely debated and there are violent outbursts among the crew. Threats and accusations fly.

5:05 pm:

After much argument and some minor scuffling among the crew, the decision is made to hike up to meet Mr. Compton. If he has duped us into climbing this far with no sign of Dodecatheon utahensis he will surely be lyched. The crew reluctantly begins the long trek up to the Sundial. There are periodic violent outbursts and many more complaints of dehydration.

5:35 pm:

Exhausted, we reach Mr. Compton. He is grinning (in a somewhat oversatisfied manner in my opinion) and has found a striking population of the Dodecatheon perched high in a seeping overhang. The evening sun glistens on the tiny streams of water descending down the rock. We rejoice, collect all our field notes and site metrics, and then take a few pictures of the newest known population of Dodecatheon utahensis. As the crags across the canyon become bathed in whiskey colored light, we wrap up, pat each other on the backs, and head back.

7:15 pm:

The crew retires to the Brighton Village Store and is rewarded by the orgnizer of this expedition with one 12 oz. beer each for their efforts. It is noted that this amounts to less than one ounce per hour of searching during the day. Laughter and much rejoicing follows.
