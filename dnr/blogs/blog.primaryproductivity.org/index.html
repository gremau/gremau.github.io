Wednesday, November 21, 2007

I am the master of my domain

I purchased a domain on the internet. Land is cheap out there on the internet these days. If you visit my domain, which is called www.primaryproductivity.org, at this exact moment you will find nothing substantive, just some links and ads from GoDaddy.com (the registrar that I purchased my name from) that serve as a kind of placeholder until I get a host and some of my own content built there. I don't know what I'll do in this domain. Probably I'll start posting my work there - so these blogs will probably move over there eventually, and hopefully a new blog on my graduate research experiences will develop, and I can keep interesting electronic media and info there for people to access. I hope it will all come together as something interesting over time.

The name.... is dorky, but I like it, and if you don't like it you should stop and think about this: Primary productivity is defined as the movement of carbon from the atmosphere into living organisms over time. Essentially, it is the rate of photosynthesis, and 99% of all life on earth depends on the products of this directly or indirectly. This process is performed mainly by vascular plants on land and by algae in the oceans. You, and all the other beings that grow and reproduce by eating plants and other animals are another result of primary productivity. So, what I'm saying here is that this blog is about YOU. And me. Its about us.

Tuesday, October 16, 2007

My life is over, but I will continue to blog about it

I have yet again postponed real life to become a student for several more years, this time a "graduate student" at the University of Utah. I'm joining an ecosystem ecology lab that focuses on studying biosphere-atmosphere interactions, the carbon cycle at multiple scales, and blah blah pollution blah feedbacks to blah-bla isotopic blablablah climate change bla-blah blah blablah. I don't know specifically what part of this research I'll be latching on to and developing into a thesis project yet, but as long as I keep sitting at my desk reading journal articles, gazing blankly out the window, reading some more, thinking about the metabolic theory of ecology, staring at the ceiling, reading all this weeks Nature articles that apply to my field, sending emails to professors, pondering, going to get coffee and then coming back to my desk for more reading, thinking, making lists of papers to read, staring, and so on, as I am currently doing almost every day of the week, I'll probably come up with something good in a few years.

So, what does that mean for this blog? I don't really know. I'll sit at my desk and think about it. One thing I do know is that I like this blog and I want to keep writing here, or at least continue writing in the same vein - that is, writing creatively about the things I do on a daily basis while bringing up the scientific thinking and processes behind it in a clear and interesting way. It helps me explain things to myself (and possibly others) and prevents me from getting desperately bored with my life in academia. I also like posting pictures.

One idea I have is to give this blog a makeover and turn it into my graduate student blog. I could archive the seed collecting blog posts somewhere, rearrange the links, change the title, make a few other minor adjustments, and then continue my blogging career. I could also just orphan this one and start a completely new blog. I don't know.

Anyways, here is a picture of an old broken-down truck sitting in a field at the Entrada field station, a new U of U research facility on the Dolores river that I visited last week.

Wednesday, May 09, 2007

We'll have a Polygala Gala...


You may remember Polygala subspinosa from last year. How could you forget? I never will. It is the most beautiful, the most elusive, the most sought after, the most frustrating plant in the world. Polygala subspinosa is my dream plant. And my nightmare.

I first met Polygala subspinosa at my Temple Mountain camp while looking at some Eriogonums and stuff. One morning, I walked around the corner of a Pinion pine and there were two very small, very beautiful plants with purple and yellow blossoms unfurling in the morning sun. That was all I needed to become completely obsessed. I spent the remainder of the summer criss-crossing the state, scrawled herbarium notes in hand, mumbling fuzzy map coordinates to myself, searching like a junkie for that one population that would go to Kew.

I never found more than half a dozen plants in one place. I would drive 50 miles out of my way, then scramble like a lemur all over an entire valley or mountainside to find one plant. One lone, beautiful plant. Taunting me. Holding those remarkable blossoms proudly above the thorny green mound below. It happened over and over and I became an utter mess. My friends stopped talking to me, my parents wrote me out of the will, I lost 30 pounds and hardly slept at all. When I finally hit rock bottom I had been dragging a plant press through the Wah Wah Mountains for 4 days without food or water and then collapsed face down in a pile of cow manure. A rancher saved me from suffocating to death and then talked some sense into me in that way that only a range-worn cowboy can. His leathery lips were glued shut most of the time, but when he chose to speak it was through complicated metaphors about the cattle, the range, the changing of seasons. It was deep, and I still don't completely understand all of what he said, but eventually I straightened out and gathered up the pieces of my shattered life, vowing never to lay eyes on another Polygala subspinosa.

But today everything changed.

Someone named Deb, the Capitol Reef NP botanist, contacted me after seeing Polygala subspinosa on my Seeds of Success list. She told me about a population that she's seen in a place called Miner's Mountain. It should be blooming soon. It might be big enough to collect.

It might be.

It could go.

This time it could go....
