The case of the head-bonking cows, revisited

Xylorhiza venusta, Buckmaster Draw, suspected selenium accumulator.

While rambling around in the San Rafael Swell last week, I found populations of a couple new species (new to me, that is) of Astragalus, my favorite plant genus, which I judged to be selenophytes - plants with a bizzare affinity for the element selenium. I could tell that this was the case using several clues. First, these plants stink pretty terrifically with a pungent garlic-like odor. Secondly, they were growing near sandstones and shales of Late-Cretaceous marine origin. Thirdly and most convincingly, while keying them out I saw that Stan Welsh calls them "selenophytes" in the Utah Flora. I could add a fourth clue that there were some listless, emaciated cattle bonking their heads into rocks nearby, if that had been the case. In fact, it was not the case, but it could very well have been the case if the cattle had been grazing these particular plants. You may recall that I posted something about the scent of locoweed and selenium a while ago, promising to elaborate later. I will now elaborate.

First lets get to know selenium. Selenium (Se) is element number 34 on the periodic table, in the same family as Sulfur and Tellurium, and here on Earth it participates in a wide and complex range of geochemical and biochemical activities. It is an essential trace nutrient in animals (including cows and people) that gets incorporated into selenoenzymes whose antioxidant properties protect cell membranes from damage by free-radicals. This is similar to what vitamin E does, and these two nutrients can complement and sometimes substitute for one another in the body. But, besides being an essential nutrient, selenium can be very toxic, and strangely, the range between having an insufficient amount of selenium and a toxic amount of selenium is quite narrow, so be careful with the selenium supplements. (Selenium links 1, 2, 3, 4)

Most plants don't ordinarily require selenium (though this varies, as we shall see) and are actually sensitive to high levels of soil Se. Under normal circumstances though, many plants do absorb small amounts of available Se from the soil and this usually provides a sufficient amount of Se to animals that eat plants (or the animals that eat these animals). In the Colorado Plateau and other parts of the western U.S. however, the soils are often substantially higher in selenium than in many places in the world. Occasionally this creates some water quality issues for people, but it creates more of a problem for livestock and the livestock industry due to the existence of selenophytes and "locoweeds."

Astragalus woodruffii, near Hanksville, UT, exhibiting selenophytic behavior

As a result of this unique and hostile soil chemistry, many plants of the west have adapted to tolerate, and even thrive in highly seleniferous areas. The livestock that graze these plants wouldn't ordinarily be under threat, since most plants don't absorb much Se. However, some plants, most notably those in the genuses Astragalus, Stanleya, and Xylorhiza have gone a step past tolerance and evolved into selenium bioaccumulators or even "hyperaccumulators" that actively absorb amounts of Se that are far in excess of what can be toxic to other organisms. The reasons for this are unclear, but some research suggests that the high levels of this element can help protect the plants from fungal infections or herbivory by insects and mammals (Plant defense links 1, 2, and a pdf).

The odor of selenophytes has been variously described as garlicky, snake-like (?), like sulfurey-garlic, or like snakes stir-fried in garlic with sulfur sauce, and is probably due to the plants volatalizing selenium as dimethylselenide, which has a very pungent odor. I would guess that this smell would be a warning to herbivores not to eat the plant. Unfortunately, livestock don't always pay attention, especially when there isn't other forage around, and end up with "alkali disease" or the "blind staggers," two older names for selenium poisoning.

There are 2 other types of poisoning that exist in the genus Astragalus besides the selenium kind. True locoweeds include many plants in Astragalus and Oxytropis (including some selenium accumulators) that contain swainsonine, a potent toxin that causes something called lysosomal storage disease. So, even if the Astragalus doesn't smell like dimethylselenide, it can still be poisonous to livestock, or people. The name "locoweed" is, of course, derived from the effects that poisoning by these plants have on livestock behavior, well described here and here.

So, this explains why some plants get BO in the Utah desert, and why the cattle that eat them are going berserk. But, wherefore all the selenium, and where will it go when the plants decay? More on the biogeochemical cycling of Se, next time, on BlogNova.

Casting ideas for the screenplay I am writing

I am currently writing a screenplay that will be cast primarily with plants. Those of you who watched Lancelot Link: Secret Chimp (which, inexplicably, hasn't been made into a feature length film yet) will appreciate the advantages and potential hilarity of casting a movie without any Homo sapiens. Plants, like chimps, will never demand all the extravagent luxuries, such as being paid, that human actors do.

Anyways, my screenplay is the story of one plant and that plant's loyal, simpleton pals turning back a relentless tide of non-native invaders into their ecosystem. I haven't written the entire thing yet, but basically I see it as an against-all-odds triumph over adversity, an inspiring story of a community of organisms coming together to defeat an army of evil invaders, and a tender interspecies romance that was doomed from the start due to forces beyond their control. It is based on a true story that is being played out every day in ecosystems all over the planet, so I think that the film will really reach people, even though there are no people in the screenplay. After all, we inhabit the same ecosystems that plants do. I am pitching it to Miramax and a few other major studios in the fall. Those of you who think this is a joke won't be invited to the glitzy Hollywood premier.

The setting: The interface between a large urban area and a unique natural area, development and careless gardening run rampant, not at all unlike the western foothills of the Wasatch mountains.

The cast of characters:

The hero of our story is a handsome, yet humble native of the ecosystem. This plant may seem simple, even naieve, but a complex mind and myriad passions rage beneath the surface. Well adapted to its environment and motivated by a fierce moral code and a painful injustice from the past, this character is a hero for everyplant.

Saturday, May 13, 2006

Rare plants of El Dorado

Senecio layneae, Ceanothus lemmonii (Layne's butterweed, Lemmon's ceanothus).

The Folsom Office of the BLM manages a network of preserves in El Dorado County, CA that include habitat for eight rare plant species. These are plants endemic to a couple of unique soil types that contain a high abundance of minerals that are often toxic to plants - those derived from something called the gabbro formation, and serpentine soils (derived from serpentinite which often contains asbestos and is California's state rock). This area is another example of how plants adapt to ecological niches confined to limited areas, leading to the phenomenon of endemism. Due in part to the high number of unique geological formations and soils in California, there is a very high level of endemism in its flora. Similar geologically based endemism also occurs in Utah.

Friday, May 12, 2006

Greg makes a triumphant return to the botanical blogosphere

I've returned to Salt Lake City after learning a great deal and meeting many good people from the BLM and some other botanical gardens around the West. Also, on my last day in Sacramento, I was able to visit one of the units of a preserve administered by the BLM (and other agencies), and see several threatened and endangered plant species, which I will be posting pictures of tomorrow.

Here are some things that I learned:

Plant Conservation Alliance

Seeds of Success exists under a large national framework of federal and non-federal plant conservation efforts called the Plant Conservation Alliance. This alliance includes many federal agencies, such as BLM, and many more non-federal entities such as botanical gardens, native plant groups, and biologists. The PCA stategizes and musters resources so that these groups can "work collectively to solve the problems of native plant extinction and native habitat restoration, ensuring the preservation of our ecosystem." I had wondered if there would ever be a cohesive movement for plant conservation such as those that support wildlife, and I'm glad that Red Butte Gardens can participate. I hope that we will continue to develop this partnership, and I hope that the PCA can withstand the era of diminishing government support for conservation efforts, especially those that aren't just conserving the most charismatic megafauna.

Vernal Pools

We spent several hours on an isolated little fragment of BLM land near our hotel that supports several small, beautiful vernal pools. We were making a practice seed collection of the weedy grass species growing on the swales because all the beautiful native wildflowers growing in the pools were too early to collect seeds from. Being preoccupied with the tasks at hand, I didn't get any good pictures of the vernal pool flowers, except for this one nice monkeyflower (Mimulus sp.)
Vernal pool habitats were once a very common feature of California's central valley, but are increasingly being cleared for the sprawling urban and agricultural development overtaking the area. These habitats occur in the undulating, but generally flat topography of the valley that is underlain with water impermeable clay soils. During winter and spring rains, water collects in the depressions in the hardpan forming shallow, ephemeral pools. These pools support an abundance of organisms, including diverse annual and perennial plants, crustaceans, and amphibians, whose life cycles are adapted to the very brief existence of their environment. Many of these plants and animals are endemic to the vernal pool habitats. As the weather warms and the pools dry out over a period of days to months, dormant crustacean eggs must hatch, grow into adulthood, mate, and lay eggs in the pool before the water evaporates. Diverse plant species play out their various life cycles in shrinking concentric rings of germination, bloom, and seed set as the level of the pools drop and the soil dries to a hardpan by summertime. Similar ephemeral pool habitats occur in many places in the world, including the slickrock potholes of Southern Utah.

Faust, Utah: Part One

Looking North from Faust RR Crossing

The town of Faust, Utah, which consists of 2 houses at a crossroads, pastures, a railroad crossing, and one historical monument, sits unnassumingly in the middle of the wide Rush Valley, about 20 miles south of Toole. I have always wondered how it got its name, and whether it might be Utah's premier location to make a pact with the devil. More recently, I have wondered whether I might find some plant populations worth collecting there. So, today I set out to answer both questions.

First, I did a little research on the history of the town, and whether the origin of its name has any similarity, real or imagined, to the old tale of Dr. Faust. I found that indeed it does! Faust is the site of an old Pony Express stop that was in use in 1860-1861. It was operated by one "Doc" Faust, who was a German immigrant. He was also a dubious scholar and a seeker of wealth and recognition: he abandoned his medical school studies to join the California gold rush. Failing to strike it rich, he returned to Utah, where he had previously joined the Mormon Church and was made the keeper of the mail stop in Rush Valley. He spent many years here, before and after the Pony Express years, raising cattle and horses. Jaromy Jessop, of the Toole Transcript Bulletin, offers a longer account of Doc Faust's life here. By all accounts Faust was a faithful and decent man, admired for his kind treatment of Native American peoples in the area, and probably never sold his soul to the devil. However, he did sell his land and stock to Porter Rockwell, the original "Destroying Angel of Mormondom," when he retired.

Armed with this knowledge, I drove out to Faust (searching, searching, searching for plants in the hot sun the whole way) and conducted a series of experiments to see whether the sale of my soul would result in even one cold beer. A summary of the results:
  • Standing at the crossroads and clearly stating "I would give my immortal soul for a cold beer". Result: 0 beers (p =0.002)
  • Standing at the pasture gate and saying to a black horse (likeliest candidate to be a Mephistopheles) "Devilhorse! I'll give you my soul for one cold beer." Result: 0 beers, but still inconclusive... the horse did look up.
  • The item below was found near the site of experiment 2.

Thursday, May 04, 2006

Crazy couplets; A whiff of locoweed

Phlox ???
Spent forever at the herbarium today puzzling over the plants in my press. I was especially puzzled over this little Phlox which is very common along the roadsides, and in disturbed areas Emery and Carbon counties. It is either Phlox hoodii, or Phlox austromontana, but the herbarium specimens look identical to me, and the dichotomies made in the key couplets are ridiculously vague (how open or compact is the plant?). I suspect that I will have to decide what kind of hairs it has (glandular or not?), and where exactly the hairs are. Thats the only difference I can see, and I may even be imagining it. Suggestions anyone?

Happily though, before being stumped, I keyed out several other plants, including 3 Astragalus. Two of these were with no small amount of help from Eddy (RBG Plant Records curator). Thanks Eddy. We now have Astragalus mollissimus, A. praelongus, A. preussii, and Xylorhiza venusta (Cisco woodyaster) populations nailed down, though I'm not sure which of these we will collect. All three of these Astragalus species can be considered "locoweeds," which means they are dangerous to livestock. The latter 3 (or maybe all 4) are selenium indicator species, or "selenophytes," meaning, among other things, that they smell terrible and the press is best left in the garage when drying such vile plants. As you might have guessed, the toxicity, the selenium, and the smell are all related. Due to the unique geologic makeup of the area, these are common characteristics of plants living in the San Rafael Swell and the Colorado Plateau as a whole, and they have implications for things like agriculture and range/resource management. I'll be posting more on this topic later.
