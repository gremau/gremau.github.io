Frasera albomarginata: You are one weird looking plant. I couldn't figure out what you were for the longest time. Next year we'll give you more time to set seed.

Abronia fragrans and nana: You two are great. I'm sure the perfect Sand verbena year is coming before the apocalypse, and when it does, I expect the San Rafael Desert and the DER to be blanketed in in your blossoms.

The winds of change just blew sand in my eyes

San Rafael Desert dunes stabilized by Poliomintha incana and Quercus welshii.

Good evening all, a few weeks ago I made passing reference to plants that stabilize sand dunes and how important they are. I was referring, in part, to our lovely Poliomintha incana species. And though that plant may elude us as a Millenium Seed Bank species due to lackluster seed production and a lack of manpower this year, I still think it is important to talk about why Poliomintha incana and other sand stabilizing plants are becoming so intriguing in these crazy times that we live in. So, gather all your tender minds around the monitor - its time for the POVHAD blog to teach you some stuff.

In the aforementioned post I posted a link to an NPR feature on windblown dust in the Southwest. The feature talked about how, particularly in Utah, airborne dust is on the rise due to the decline of biotic soil crusts. These soil crusts, which stabilize large areas of fine textured soils in Utah, are in decline mainly due to human activites such as ORV use and grazing activity, and the resulting increases in blowing dust has a variety of problematic consequences. (USGS soil crust site). Well, a close relative of windblown dust, is windblown sand, and as I was taking cowardly refuge in my pickup truck during a severe San Rafael Desert sandstorm I was inspired to learn more about windblown sand and the plant communities growing in this sandblasted ecosystem.

* * *

Unknown, San Rafael Desert.

For the purpose of this discussion, let's talk about what is needed to make sand dunes, and then define two types of sand dunes. The two critical factors that make dunes possible are a plentiful source of sand, and strong, frequent winds to blow the sand. There are many geologic formations that weather into sand, (usually sandstones) and no shortage of wind in many parts of the arid West, making this a good place for dunes. The first type of dunes are active sand dunes - large areas of windblown (or eolian) sands that are actively moving. Though such dunes may not appear to move, perhaps remaining in a natural depression like a valley, the sand nevertheless moves with each wind storm to form fresh patterns and features in the dunes. The physical aspects of active sand dune formation and movement are discussed here. In an active dune environment, the constant churning of sand allows little chance for vegetation to establish and makes these areas unsuitable for human habitation, agriculture and grazing. The second dune type - stabilized dunes - are large areas of surface sand, often in sheets, that are not actively moving or changing shape, at least not on a large scale. The reason for these dunes' stability is that they support the growth of vegetation. Not just little annuals either, but strongly rooted perennials and shrubs. Though the wind may blow, the plants that grow here can hold on and anchor a well developed community of organisms.

Active dunes are common in the the Sonoran, Mojave, and Chihuahuan deserts of North America, but with a few exceptions, there aren't many active sand dunes on the Colorado Plateau. This is in spite of the fact that places like the San Rafael Desert have both an abundant source of sand - the layercake sandstones of the region thrust out of the ground everywhere - and reliable strong winds. I have rarely visited Hanksville or Moab without griping for at least a few hours in stiff to howling winds. However, the dunes here are almost always stabilized by vegetation. The difference, as you might have guessed, is moisture. The Colorado Plateau recieves just slightly more moisture than the deserts mentioned above, allowing plants to gain a solid foothold in the sand.

I counted eleven unique insect species on this one milkweed plant.

The dunes of the San Rafael Desert are stabilized by a diverse community of beautiful and unique plants and it is well worth a visit during the spring or early summer. In addition, there is a huge diversity of insects and larger wildlife such as lizards, rabbits, prarie dogs, and pronghorn antelope. A few of the interesting plants you might find there:

Amsonia tomentosa - Wooly Amsonia
Artemisia filifolia - Old man sagebrush
Quercus welshii - a gambellii/turbinella hybrid that stabilizes dunes.
Ephedra sp. - Mormon tea
Wyethia scabra - Rough mulear sunflower
A wild assortment of Milkweeds, Flax, Phacelia, Asters, Globemallows, Astragalus, Lupine, Penstemon, and Sand verbenas.
And of course our lovely Poliomintha incana

So, asking what the future holds for a stabilized dune ecosystem that recieves only a fraction more moisture on average than nearby active dune ecosystem seems like a relevant question to ask, especially in the era of human caused climate change. The USGS seems to think that there is a potential for large areas of stable dunes in the southwest to become active again, perhaps rendering large sections of Navajo and Hopi land in the Four Corners region uninhabitable (USGS site.) According to them, even a slight drying of the climate might make plant communities such as the one in the San Rafael Desert recede, leaving large tracts of active dunes. Such a phenomenon is already a part of widespread desertification happening in northern China, Hopefully, we will be able to manage such a crisis before it gets so bad, and learning more about dune stabilizing plants might be a small part of that. Incidentally, 2006 is the International Year of Deserts and Desertification.

* * *

Before the sky turned a murky brown and I scrambledblindly into the truck I saw great clouds of sand, lit yellow from behind, rise out of the valley between Wild Horse Butte and the Reef. I watched all the shrubs and bunchgrasses rolling over under waves of wind, and then hunker down to the ground to endure a good sandblasting. My truck started to shake back and forth. It was beautiful.

San Rafael Desert, Gilson Buttes and the Henries in distance.

What do I keep in my long, slender, damselfly abdomen?


a. Sewing needle

b. Cotton swab

c. Primary and secondary genitalia structures

d. Caudal lamellae




answer: c and d.

Caudal lamellae are respiratory/ventilatory structures present on the abdomen in the aquatic larval stage of a damselfly.

During mating, male damselflies curl their abdomens forward to deposit sperm on an external secondary genitalia structure. The male then clasps the female damselfly to his body and the two fly around in this coupled formation, often for several hours. In this time she curls her abdomen forward to pick up the male's sperm and fertilize her eggs. When mating is over, the pair may fly in tandem until the female can deposit eggs into aquatic vegetation or directly into the water.

The couples I saw were too shy to land and let me take a picture to publish on the internet.
