Wednesday, January 30, 2008

No more moon missions

in rings of orbiting desk chairs, grown men
not known to known men

the darkness makes them cringe, perhaps caught
bespectacled, impeccable, palms hot

back here the terminals blinking, blame them
don't try to tame them

the grainy lander camera sends back scenes
of tiny hallways and vending machines

swept away the footprints of old, gray men
you'll never tame them

Friday, December 07, 2007

carapace


We found brown dormant grasses tilting
sideways along the shore, drift laid on
spongy gravel we walked softly over.
Low-angle sun scattering west to east
lit forking tracks in mud the feeding geese had made.
We walked silent miles in view of October mountains
only to emerge upon this scene:

An abandoned ice chest holding bait,
and a blue plastic bag floating in water.
Two folding chairs face the lake. Was a time
this meant abduction, murder, intrigue...
Knowing abandonment, we no longer concern ourselves
with chairs unfilled with supervising adults, or children
not pulling cold sodas from the chest.

A short time later, and reaching a point,
the shore curves back and leaves us standing
in the flat yellow rush of twilight
streaming into the dark half of the sky.
Guessing the bathymetry, casting in, then tipping back
the second half of a bottle, legs crossed in wool pants,
we reconstruct events from fragments.

Once startled by flashing trout in normally
dead fetid canals, I took dusk wading
expeditions after coming home from school
in the wandering pickup. Reasoning,
in bed, against warm parental breath laced with fetid mint
persuaded me that right now I was scavenging strays
washed down from upstream havens.

If we went to the same school, would we
have set out like Lewis and Clark and together
unearthed glinting chrysalides from beds upstream?
Or been brought home scowling and stronger,
convinced each was incomprehensible to the other.
Not quite a choice, just the time in our lives when souls
were condensing into metal plates.

We pause to catch and clean one rainbow
and have no further luck with trout.
Our limbs are lean, long and brittle now,
draped in dusty, tattered plaid and we squat,
with our long matted beards gathered between our legs.
The red label bottle has one unwanted swig and we see
something scuttle for our one caught fish.

We observe the movement in lantern light,
watery shadows lurching hungrily
among lake floor stones. Amused and peering
out of gray sockets edged with joyful crowsfeet,
we have ideas for those drawn to clean floating rainbows.
Soon, faint twilight streams into our clicking bucket
onto ruddy shells that shine like wet steel.

We'll eat what we catch, even the one
caught trout we used as bait. On the lake
the moon emerges through the surface,
its weathered and pockmarked patina bulging
like the ancient carapace of a grandfather crayfish
without concern for the offspring we've scavenged in silence
and are finally bringing home.

Wednesday, April 04, 2007

this is what we do on my sister's birthday

We go to the Olive Garden.

We sit down in the booth that we requested and the server asks us if we want anything to drink, so we order something to drink. The server leaves and we talk for a few minutes until the server returns with our drinks. The server is a nice young woman and she also gives us a bowl of salad and a basket of breadsticks. We look around at the other Olive Garden patrons and see that they are eating. They are looking at their food. They are not looking at us. They don't see the two of us pull a garbage bag out of our pockets. We open the garbage bags under the table and casually pour the salad and breadsticks into the bags. Tonight I am filling mine with breadsticks and my sister is filling hers with the salad. We continue talking and drinking our drinks until the server returns, at which time we tell her that we haven't decided on an entree but we would like more salad and breadsticks. She brings it, and we do the same thing - pour them into the bag. In a few more minutes she comes back and we order some cheap wine and more salad and breadsticks. So she brings back the wine, salad, and breadsticks. We drink the wine and put the rest in the bag. When she comes back we finally order an entree and say that we would like more salad and breadsticks. We always say please, and she seems to be OK with all the salad and breadsticks we are ordering. She comes back shortly with the salad and breadsticks. She has to; it's her job. This goes on for a while. We order salad and breadsticks several more times before our meals come, and while we are picking at our meals we order even more salad and breadsticks. We also order salad and breadsticks with our dessert and again with our coffee. The bags are getting pretty full by the end of the night.

Somehow we managed to drag out the huge bags without anyone noticing.
Throwing handfuls of salad off the side.
Then the cops showed up.
We ran.
Salad and breadsticks everywhere.
Sliding around on salad dressing.
More stuff happens.
Salad and breadsticks.
Food.

HER: Lets go have some fun with our friends.

ME: I feel horrible.

Thursday, February 08, 2007

ellesmere island

Had all this incoming radiation
been striking a larger area,
it might have made all the difference.
Glancing angles offer less intensity,
but allow one to absorb more
in the course of a day.

Our aspect was obviously too direct
and we were melting by noon.

The Law of Cosines
is not in itself meaningful,
but as we are now
a pair of low-latitude puddles,
we're inclined to make meaning of it.

So then, we agree on meaninglessness,
but there is still a reason that spring
in Ecuador is so different
than spring on Ellesmere.
