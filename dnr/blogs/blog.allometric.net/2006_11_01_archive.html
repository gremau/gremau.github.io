Nobody's perfect.
There was never a perfect person around.
You just got half devil and half angel in you

She promised herself she'd lead a good life from now on.
She blamed it all on herself.
She didnt care if she was happy or not,
she just wanted to make up for what she did wrong.

The sun looks ghostly when there's a mist on the river
and everything's quiet. I never knowed it before.
An' you could see people on the shore,
but it was far off and you couldn't see what they were doin.
They were probly callin for help or somethin,

shadows of guys comin at you an' stuff.

that was the first time.

cold hands touchin the back of my neck and,
and it could be the dead coming for me or something.
I remember this guy his name was Blackjack, he died.
He only had one leg and he died,
and I think that was Blackjack
makin those noises.

back to the lake


we were fixing a flat in green river when he went down
we heard the crash of diving flukes
felt the stillness grow

we went out around the town smashing old clawfoot bathtubs
stealing some things and walked into
a secret pigeon roost

it was one floor above state liquors, all along the walls
were rows of movie seats in storage
painted white as snow

our footsteps crackled and every sentence mingled strangely
with flapping sounds, it didn't matter
which words we used

across the street all the cues were crooked but they dont charge
for the game if you keep buying drinks
we amused some locals

they took us to their old trailer on the outskirts of town
and we waited for something new to come
from the bottle or the bonfire

we'll never remember their names, woke up in storm thick air
with raindrops pattering softly
sizzling on last nights coals

we put on our shoes, shook the dust out of our clothes and hair
and wound our way through the greasewood
to ned's house of tires


* * *


driving along the base of the book cliffs if felt like we were floating outside of the car, just hangin' up there in space tethered to the bumper. we looked around and tried to find old miners' cabins in the cliffs, abandoned corrals, buzzards picking at old carcasses, or pronghorns out racing each other all over those bone dry plains between woodside and price. we never really saw much that we could tell what it was, and we mostly were just trying to keep our minds busy. just flappin' around up there, never gettin' caught on the girders below an overpass or anything, all the way back to the lake.

Saturday, November 04, 2006

perch

My new computer dominates.

* * * * * * * * * * * *

i built a new computer
that was much more powerful than I expected
so i began to fear it

it sat under my desk droning seductively
but i couldnt think
of ways to put its sleek silicon to work

i was intimidated
by the number of calculations it needed to do
to be used to its potential

all i really wanted to do was to check my email
create some text files
and a spreadsheet with names and numbers of all my friends

i released it into the jordan river
and watched it float downstream to find its special purpose
and reach for the brass ring

twelve years later i was out spearfishing for marsh suckers
my beard had grown long
the october brine flies were buzzing in waves

i saw an angular shape rising
over the grey mud flats and low clumps of iodinebush
it was my powerful computer

i went to see what it had been up to all these years
it looked older
tiny patterns in pink salt crystals covered the base

and the top edge of the case
was worn into a rough curve and painted white from years
of being used as a perch

Bonneville


You think you are under pressure now?

Back in the Pleistocene, the space you now occupy was under 1000 feet of fresh water. The valleys we live in, from here to southern Idaho, bulging west beyond Wendover, NV, and south past tiny Milford, UT, were all lake floors. There were thousands of feet of water pushing down on every living thing in this place.

Imagine living under that kind of pressure. Thankfully, about 15,000 years ago the dam holding back all this water broke and Lake Bonneville flooded north into Idaho.

Today, with the heaviness lifted, the former lake floors we walk, run, and jump on are free of all that pressure and are reacting appropriately. The earth's crust is rising, slowly, in a process called isostatic rebound. Its been going on ever since the dam broke and the rate of rebound is now slowing down.

Every time your feet leave the ground you travel an airborn arc over the earth and reach an apex that defines the greatest distance you will be from the earth. At this apex you unconciously predict where the ground will be when you land. Your brain calculates a location below where mother earth is waiting and your muscles prepare for the impact to come at a particular time.

Around here, the deceleration of isostatic rebound means that your calculation is always a little off. Because the earth is rising to meet you more slowly than it was when you jumped off of it, you will remain airborn for a fraction of a second longer than you calculated at your apex, causing your heart to drop into your guts for a disconcerting moment. Then the earth will solidly strike your feet just barely after you expect it to, imparting a subtle sense of the unreal to the rest of your life.
