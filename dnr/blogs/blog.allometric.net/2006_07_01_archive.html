Friday, July 28, 2006

My happiest day


For once I was feeling good when I woke up
I was wearing a big smile, and the smile kept growing
Throughout the day I kept smiling bigger and bigger

Pretty soon the smile was knocking stuff of my desk
And cracking the pictures on the walls of my little room
So I turned sideways and squeezed through the door

And went out to the front lawn to get some air
The sun and breezes felt good, and I smiled even more
The people walking down my street to the park

Were looking a little worried about my giant grin
Rocking back and forth out on the front lawn in the sun
The dog got nervous, started barking, and ran off

I smiled bigger and the powerlines started to snap
Mothers gathered up the children in their arms and ran
Buildings started to topple under my huge lower lip

The interstate clogged with cars evacuating the city
Soon it got dark and started to rain, but I was still happy
So my smile stretched even wider across the city

It was rocking back and forth across the whole valley
Choppers were circling and shining spotlights through the rain
And there were copcars with their lights on all around

A bunch of sirens were wailing and horns were honking
All the lights were reflecting off of my big wet lips and teeth
And people in the choppers shouted with megaphones

One megaphone was shouting about depressing things
Like dead puppies, despair, cancer, politics, franchises, and war
Another was ex-girlfriends complaining about stuff I did

But I kept smiling anyways
I couldn't help it
I was happy

Friday, July 14, 2006

I drive frozen foods



22 thousand individually wrapped
bean and cheese burritos across the Mojave

50 thousand pounds of frozen
corn niblets through the Chihuahuan desert

18 thousand cans of frozen orange
juice concentrate across the Great Basin

30 thousand pounds of heat 'n serve
chicken tenders over the Sonoran

Icy mist drifts over my boots when I open the gate
My sweetheart left for a Zamboni driver

*

Its 125 degrees on the asphalt. The cooler is running like a stallion. Today I pulled a trailer full of neapolitan icecream sandwiches from Tulsa to Phoenix. I haul more frozen fishsticks and mixed vegetables over more miles of these shimmering blacktop mirages than Jesus himself. And when my cargo reaches your kitchen, the peas still pour miraculously out of the bag like frosted green marbles. One might even roll and fall between the countertop and fridge and land by the ancient tater-tot.
